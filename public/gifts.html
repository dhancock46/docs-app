Providing for Children from a Prior Relationship

If you have children from a previous marriage or relationship, you face a difficult choice: leaving them money means less for your current spouse and children, but leaving them nothing feels wrong.

A Solution: A nominal trust created in your will and funded by term life insurance

Create a trust funded with a small amount (like $100) then purchase term life insurance to fund the balance of the trust.

This way:

  • Your children from the prior relationship receive something meaningful
  • Your current family keeps your existing assets
  • The life insurance provides the real funding without reducing what your spouse and current children inherit

This approach lets you care for all your children without penalizing your current family.

Specific Gifts (Optional)

These are particular items or small amounts for specific people (e.g., "$5,000 to my nephew" or "my car to my daughter"). Most users don't make specific gifts but if you have a specific item or cash that you want to give to someone, this is where you can do that.

Remaining Estate (Where Your Main Distribution Goes)

This is the bulk of your estate - typically 90-100% of your wealth including your cash, your home, investments and any other property you own.

Example: If your estate is worth $500,000 and you make a $10,000 specific gift, your remaining estate of $490,000 goes to your primary beneficiaries.

Bottom line: Focus on the "Remaining Estate" section - that's where you'll distribute most of your wealth to your spouse, children, or other main beneficiaries.

Gift of Interest in Spouse's Retirement Accounts

In Texas, both spouses automatically own half of any retirement savings earned during marriage, regardless of who actually earned the money or whose name is on the account. This gift transfers the deceased spouse's interest in the surviving spouse's retirement accounts to the surviving spouse, so the survivor ends up owning 100% of the surviving spouse's retirement accounts.
